Statistical Independence

Statistical independence between two events, A and B, means that the occurrence of one event does not provide any information about the occurrence of the other. In probability theory, this concept is formalized by saying that events A and B are independent if:

\[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) \]

where:

  • P(A) is the probability of event A,
  • P(B) is the probability of event B,
  • P(A ∩ B) is the probability of both events occurring together (their intersection).

Illustrative Example: Rolling Dice

Consider rolling two six-sided dice. Define events:

  • A: The first die shows a 4.
  • B: The second die shows an even number.

Since the outcome of one die roll does not influence the outcome of the other, events A and B are independent. We can verify this:

\[ P(A) = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(B) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \]

The probability of both A and B occurring is:

\[ P(A \cap B) = P(\text{first die shows 4 and second die is even}) = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{12} \]

Thus, P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B), confirming that A and B are independent.

Random Variables and Independence

For random variables, independence means that the joint distribution of the variables can be separated into the product of their individual distributions.

Example: Independent Random Variables

Consider two independent random variables X and Y, where:

  • X represents the outcome of a fair six-sided die, taking values 1, 2, ..., 6,
  • Y represents the outcome of a fair coin flip, taking values 0 (tails) or 1 (heads).

The probability distribution for X is:

\[ P(X = x) = \frac{1}{6} \quad \text{for } x = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 \]

The probability distribution for Y is:

\[ P(Y = y) = \frac{1}{2} \quad \text{for } y = 0 \text{ or } 1 \]

Since X and Y are independent, the probability that X = x and Y = y can be calculated as:

\[ P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{12} \]

Conditional Independence

Two events can also be conditionally independent given a third event. This means that, given the third event, the occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of the other.

Example: Conditional Independence in Disease Testing

Suppose we are testing for a disease. Define the following events:

  • D: A person has the disease.
  • T: The test result is positive.
  • S: The person is a smoker.

If T and S are conditionally independent given D, then knowing whether a person smokes does not affect the test result for the disease if we already know whether they have the disease.

\[ P(T \cap S \mid D) = P(T \mid D) \cdot P(S \mid D) \]

Independent and Identically Distributed (i.i.d.) Random Variables

Independence is also essential in defining a collection of random variables as independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). This property is key to many statistical theories, such as the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem.

Example: Repeated Coin Tosses

If we toss a fair coin multiple times, each outcome (heads or tails) can be considered as a random variable. Let:

  • X1, X2, ..., Xn be the outcomes of n coin tosses, where each Xi is a random variable that takes the value 1 for heads and 0 for tails.

These random variables are i.i.d., meaning:

  1. Each Xi has the same probability distribution: \[ P(X_i = 1) = \frac{1}{2} \] and \[ P(X_i = 0) = \frac{1}{2} \].
  2. They are independent: the outcome of one toss does not affect the outcome of another.
