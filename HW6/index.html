Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and Its Relationship with Density Functions and Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDFs)

1. Recall of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) connects differentiation and integration in two key parts:

  • First Part: If \( f \) is a continuous function on an interval \([a, b]\), and \( F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt \), then \[ F'(x) = f(x). \]
  • Second Part: If \( F \) is any antiderivative of \( f \) on \([a, b]\), then \[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a). \]

In essence, this theorem allows us to compute the integral of a function by knowing an antiderivative, and to find that integration and differentiation are inverse processes.

2. Connection to Density Functions and Cumulative Distribution Functions (CDFs)

In probability and statistics, the FTC helps describe the relationship between probability density functions (PDFs) and cumulative distribution functions (CDFs).

  • Density Function (PDF): If \( X \) is a continuous random variable with probability density function \( f(x) \), then the CDF \( F(x) \) of \( X \) is given by \[ F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt. \] Here, \( F(x) \) represents the probability that the random variable \( X \) takes on a value less than or equal to \( x \).
  • Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF): Using the FTC, we see that if \( F(x) \) is the CDF of a continuous random variable with PDF \( f(x) \), then the derivative of \( F(x) \) gives the PDF: \[ F'(x) = f(x). \] Thus, \( f(x) \) can be derived from \( F(x) \) by differentiating the CDF.

3. Using the FTC in Probability Problems

  • Probabilities as Areas: In probability, calculating the probability of a random variable falling within a range (e.g., \( P(a \leq X \leq b) \)) is equivalent to finding the area under the PDF curve from \( a \) to \( b \), which we do with integration: \[ P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a). \]
  • Applications: The FTC is frequently applied in problems that require finding probabilities, expected values, or variances for continuous random variables by integrating the PDF.
